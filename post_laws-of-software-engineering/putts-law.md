# Putt's Law

**Definition:** Technology tends to be dominated by two kinds of people: those who understand what they do not manage, and those who manage what they do not understand.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- Deep technical skill and managerial skill are rarely present in the same person at the same time.
- Non-technical managers often set unrealistic scope; isolated engineers often build technically correct but strategically wrong things.
- Hierarchies drift toward "competence inversion," where authority increases as hands-on understanding decreases.
- Technical literacy in leadership and leadership literacy in technical roles are both worth investing in.
- Bridge roles — staff engineers, CTOs, tech leads — exist to paper over this gap.

## Why it matters
Putt's Law is cynical but useful. It names a recurring friction: the person who could make a good call about a technical tradeoff isn't in the room, and the person in the room making the call can't fully evaluate it. The result is commitments that engineers can't keep and technical decisions that business can't use.

It also explains why individual frustration with "management" is often not about specific people — it's a structural outcome of how knowledge and authority get separated as organizations grow. Fixing it requires intentional design, not just better hiring.

## Examples
- **Impossible commit:** A manager from a non-engineering background promises a client a complex feature in a week because it "sounds simple," leaving the team to negotiate scope down after the fact.
- **Sidelined expert:** A senior infrastructure engineer knows exactly what needs to happen to scale the platform but has no say in budget or roadmap — so the decision gets made without the one person who understands it.

## When to apply
Pull Putt's Law out when you see chronic mismatches between what leadership expects and what engineering delivers, or when strategic technical decisions are consistently made without technical input. It's a signal to invest in bridge roles and in forums (RFCs, architecture reviews) where technical people can influence decisions before they're committed to.

Also useful when coaching engineers who are frustrated by their org — it reframes the problem as structural and points at actionable fixes rather than personal grievance.
