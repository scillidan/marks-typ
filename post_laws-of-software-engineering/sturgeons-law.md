# Sturgeon's Law

**Definition:** Ninety percent of everything is mediocre; exceptional work drives most of the value.

**Category:** Quality

## Takeaways
- Most outputs underperform; a small fraction generates disproportionate impact.
- Treating all work as equally valuable drowns good work in noise and slows the team.
- A "10x engineer" is often someone who picks the right 10% to do, not someone who types ten times faster.
- Identifying signal vs. noise is as important as producing more of either.
- Accepting the distribution reduces false urgency and sharpens priorities.

## Why it matters
Sci-fi author Theodore Sturgeon coined the line in 1957 defending his genre: yes, most of it is crap, but so is most of everything - what matters is the 10% that is great. The software corollary is that most features, code, meetings, and ideas contribute little; a few shape outcomes.

The hard part isn't admitting this abstractly; it's acting on it. Teams that treat every feature as equally deserving of polish, every meeting as equally essential, and every proposal as equally serious create enormous drag. Teams that ruthlessly separate vital work from filler move faster and build better products.

## Examples
- **Feature usage curve:** A product with 100 features typically sees heavy engagement on 10 and near-silence on 90 - and those 10 deserve most of the investment.
- **Proposal funnels:** Of a quarter's project pitches, most underdeliver; a few become the year's defining work. Planning should reflect that distribution.

## When to apply
Invoke Sturgeon's Law during roadmap planning, feature-deprecation reviews, meeting audits, and hiring calibration. It's also useful when pushing back on "we must treat everything equally" pressure - that stance usually means underinvesting in what matters and overinvesting in what doesn't.
