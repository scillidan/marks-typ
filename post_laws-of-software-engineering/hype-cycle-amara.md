# The Hype Cycle & Amara's Law

**Definition:** People overestimate a new technology's impact in the short term and underestimate it in the long term — so adoption follows a boom, bust, and slow-maturation curve.

**Category:** Decisions

## Takeaways
- Peak hype almost never matches peak usefulness; real value tends to arrive after the buzz fades.
- Be skeptical of tech at the top of the curve and open-minded about tech that's "boring" again.
- Adopt new tools to solve actual problems, not to appear current.
- The cycle repeats — today's "overhyped" is often tomorrow's quiet workhorse.

## Why it matters
Every few years some technology — microservices, blockchain, serverless, AI copilots — gets anointed as the answer to everything. Teams that adopt at peak hype often pay for a use case that isn't real yet, then bail out during the inevitable disillusionment. Meanwhile, the technology quietly matures, finds its real sweet spot, and delivers more value than anyone predicted — to the teams patient enough to wait.

Amara's Law reframes this: the short-term overshoot and the long-term undershoot are two sides of the same bias. Good engineering judgment means decoupling adoption from the hype curve and matching tools to concrete problems instead.

## Examples
- **AI:** Bold 1960s-70s predictions about general intelligence collapsed into an "AI winter." Decades of slower progress eventually produced today's genuinely transformative systems — delivering far more than the skeptics expected, just much later than the boosters promised.
- **Microservices:** Sold as a universal scaling fix, they turned out to add serious operational complexity. Mature practice now treats them as the right answer for specific contexts, not a default architecture.

## When to apply
Use this during technology selection, architecture reviews, and roadmap planning — especially when a proposal leans on how new or trendy something is. Ask: what problem are we solving that this specifically fixes, and what would we pick if we optimized for proven, stable, and cheap?

Also helpful in the opposite direction: don't dismiss a technology just because the hype faded. The post-trough phase is often when it's genuinely useful.
