# Price's Law

**Definition:** In any sizable group, about half of the useful output comes from roughly the square root of the number of contributors.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- In a 100-person team, ~10 people typically produce half the value.
- The effect is steeper than Pareto's 80/20 and sharpens as the group grows.
- Losing a top contributor is disproportionately painful because their output doesn't redistribute.
- Headcount growth does not translate linearly to output growth.
- Retention of high-impact people matters more than hiring volume.

## Why it matters
Price studied scientific publication and found that a small core of researchers produced most of the papers. The same shape shows up in engineering orgs, open-source projects, and support teams: a thin slice of the team drives most of the measurable output, while the majority handle steady-state and supporting work.

This cuts against the mental model where team output scales with headcount. It also explains why some layoffs appear survivable on paper and catastrophic in practice — if you lose the square-root people, half of real output walks out the door.

## Examples
- **Open-source commits:** A project with 30 regular contributors often has a handful (~5) who write the bulk of the substantive code, while the long tail handles docs, small fixes, and issue triage.
- **Post-acquisition shock:** A large tech company cuts staff broadly and discovers critical systems degrading, because the cuts hit the small group who actually knew how to keep them running.

## When to apply
Use Price's Law during layoff planning, re-orgs, and succession planning. Before you reshuffle, explicitly ask: who are the square-root people for this product, and are their responsibilities mirrored or documented anywhere? If not, that's the real risk.

It also sharpens hiring decisions. When choosing between "one excellent hire" and "three solid hires," Price's Law biases toward quality for high-leverage work — while being careful not to demoralize the long tail of contributors who keep the lights on.
