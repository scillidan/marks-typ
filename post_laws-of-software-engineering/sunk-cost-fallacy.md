# Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Definition:** The tendency to keep investing in a failing effort because of what's already been spent, even when walking away is clearly the better choice going forward.

**Category:** Decisions

## Takeaways
- Money, time, and effort already spent can't be recovered; they shouldn't influence what you do next.
- Good decisions weigh future cost vs. future value, independent of history.
- Set explicit checkpoints and kill criteria before you start, so reassessment doesn't feel like surrender.
- Teams need psychological safety to admit a project isn't working without looking like they failed.

## Why it matters
Software teams routinely pour more time into a struggling rewrite, a broken vendor integration, or a feature no one uses, because "we're already 80% done." The fallacy is treating past investment as a reason to continue rather than as information about viability.

The healthier frame: at any moment, the only question is whether the remaining work is worth doing compared to the alternatives. If a buggy in-house system would cost more to maintain than to replace, the previous two years of effort don't change that calculus — they're already gone either way.

## Examples
- **The doomed rewrite:** A company has spent two years building an internal CMS that's still slow and buggy. Cheaper, better commercial options exist, but leadership refuses to switch because that would "waste" the two years — locking in an even bigger waste going forward.
- **Google's graveyard:** Google routinely shuts down products that aren't earning their place, trading short-term optics for long-term portfolio health.

## When to apply
Apply this at project checkpoints, budget reviews, roadmap planning, and any moment someone argues "we can't stop now, we've put too much in." Ask explicitly: if we were starting today, knowing what we know, would we pick this path? If the answer is no, the prior investment isn't a reason — it's an anchor.

Useful in vendor selection, build-vs-buy, rewrite-vs-refactor decisions, and feature deprecation.
