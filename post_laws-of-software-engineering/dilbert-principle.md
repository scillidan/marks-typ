# Dilbert Principle

**Definition:** Organizations sometimes promote their least effective employees into management, on the theory that they'll do less damage there than in the work they were actually doing.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- Management gets treated as a dumping ground instead of a distinct discipline with its own skills.
- It sidesteps the harder conversation — underperformance — by relabeling the problem as a promotion.
- Decision quality, prioritization, and trust all erode when leadership layers fill with this pattern.
- It rests on the false premise that management is the default "up" for every career.
- Dual career tracks and honest performance management are the main countermeasures.

## Why it matters
The Dilbert Principle is the cynical sibling of the Peter Principle. Peter says promotion pushes people past their competence by accident; Dilbert says it happens on purpose, as conflict avoidance. Rather than coaching, reassigning, or parting ways with a struggling employee, the org moves them into a role where their individual output is harder to measure.

Over a few cycles, this produces management layers that engineers don't respect and that can't evaluate technical work. The compounding cost shows up as poor decisions, attrition of strong ICs who don't want to report into that, and a growing cultural belief that "promotion" doesn't actually mean anything.

## Examples
- **Promoted-out-of-code:** An engineer who consistently misses deadlines and ships fragile code gets promoted to architect or team lead. The codebase improves because they touch it less, but now they're shaping decisions for people who outperformed them.
- **Failing lead kicked upstairs:** A project lead who can't deliver is moved into a staff management role instead of being coached or let go. The technical problem becomes a people problem one level up.

## When to apply
Flag the Dilbert Principle during promotion committee discussions and performance reviews. If the argument for promoting someone sounds more like "we need to do something with them" than "they'll be great at the next role," that's the pattern. It's almost always the wrong answer for the org and for the person.

Also useful when diagnosing a middle-management layer that feels disconnected from reality — ask how those people got there, and whether the org has a healthy alternative to promotion for handling underperformance.
