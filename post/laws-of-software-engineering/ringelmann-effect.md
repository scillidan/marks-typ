# The Ringelmann Effect

**Definition:** Per-person productivity drops as a group gets larger, because coordination cost rises and individual accountability diffuses.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- When individual contributions become invisible, some people quietly coast.
- Meetings, alignment rituals, and merge conflicts eat a growing share of the day as teams grow.
- Beyond a point, adding a person reduces average throughput rather than increasing total throughput.
- Small, focused teams typically out-ship larger, fuzzily-scoped ones.
- Clear roles and visible ownership directly counteract the effect.

## Why it matters
Ringelmann ran a rope-pulling experiment and found that the pulling force per person dropped as more people joined the rope. The same dynamic shows up in engineering: ten people on one project rarely produce ten people's worth of output. Part of it is coordination overhead; part of it is social loafing — when your contribution is hard to attribute, you work less hard.

This matters because team size is one of the few levers managers feel comfortable pulling. Ringelmann says that lever is non-linear and eventually flips sign. The productive move is often to shrink the team or split the work, not to staff up.

## Examples
- **Silent brainstorm:** A 3-person design discussion has everyone actively proposing ideas. A 15-person version has two people talking and thirteen waiting for the meeting to end.
- **One module, ten devs:** Ten engineers assigned to a single module spend their days on merge conflicts, review queues, and design debates — the actual feature moves slower than it would with three people.

## When to apply
Invoke Ringelmann when you are tempted to scale a team to match a deadline, or when a team feels busy but not productive. It's especially worth raising during planning, when ownership is being assigned: if a task has more than one or two "owners," expect diluted effort.

It's also a useful lens in postmortems. If the action item is "get more people involved in reviews," consider whether that will actually improve quality or just spread responsibility so thin that no one feels it.
