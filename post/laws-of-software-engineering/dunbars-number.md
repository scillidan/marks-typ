# Dunbar's Number

**Definition:** A single person can sustain roughly 150 meaningful relationships before the social fabric has to be replaced by formal structure.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- Around 150 people, informal knowledge-sharing breaks down and hierarchy becomes necessary.
- Inside that 150, tighter layers exist: ~5 close collaborators, ~15 trusted peers, ~50 working contacts.
- The best day-to-day work happens in the small inner layers, not at the outer limit.
- Historical human groupings (villages, military companies) cluster in the same range, suggesting a cognitive cap.
- Startups hit a visible culture inflection point as they cross this threshold.

## Why it matters
Dunbar's number is less a hard cap and more a warning siren. Below it, an engineering org can run on hallway conversations, shared memory, and "ask Priya, she knows." Above it, those same habits silently fail — people make decisions in isolation because they no longer know whose toes they are stepping on.

This is why growing companies suddenly need an intranet, an RFC process, a directory, and more managers. It isn't bureaucracy for its own sake — it's the compensating infrastructure for a social graph that has outgrown individual memory.

## Examples
- **Startup at 150:** A founder-led company notices that all-hands meetings stop feeling intimate, new hires don't know what other teams do, and decisions start getting made twice. That's the Dunbar boundary being crossed.
- **Linux kernel:** Thousands of contributors ship a coherent product because the project is sliced into subsystem maintainers, each managing a Dunbar-sized circle rather than the whole community.

## When to apply
Use Dunbar's number when planning org growth, designing team topologies, or deciding when to split a team. If one manager or one Slack channel is trying to hold more than ~50 people's context, you are overdrawing the account. Split early rather than late.

It also helps when structuring communities: open-source projects, internal guilds, company-wide forums. Design for sub-Dunbar cells connected by explicit liaisons rather than assuming everyone can know everyone.
