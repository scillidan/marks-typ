# Peter Principle

**Definition:** In a hierarchy, people keep getting promoted based on their current job performance until they land in a role they're no longer good at — and stop being promoted there.

**Category:** Teams

## Takeaways
- Promotion as reward quietly assumes the next role uses the same skills as the current one, which is usually false.
- The org loses a great IC and gains a struggling manager at the same moment.
- Over time, upper layers fill with people operating at their ceiling.
- Dual career tracks let strong ICs advance without being forced into management.
- New managers need explicit training; they can't inherit the skill from being senior in the previous role.

## Why it matters
The Peter Principle is about a design flaw in how most organizations handle advancement. If the only way to get more money, scope, or prestige is to climb the people-management ladder, then the best individual contributors get pushed out of the work they were best at — into work they may not want and may not be equipped for.

The cost compounds. The organization doesn't just get a weaker manager; it also loses the output of a top engineer, and the people reporting to the new manager get a worse experience. Without an IC track, this pattern repeats every promotion cycle.

## Examples
- **The promoted ace:** A team's strongest developer is made manager. She now spends her days on 1:1s and planning she doesn't enjoy, writes less code, and neither role gets her best work.
- **QA lead mismatch:** An exceptional bug-finder becomes QA Lead, where the job is strategy, process, and headcount planning — none of which play to her strengths. The quality org weakens and she loses the work she loved.

## When to apply
Use the Peter Principle during promotion discussions, career-ladder design, and when reviewing why a team under a recently-promoted manager is struggling. Before promoting an IC into management, ask whether they actually want the role and whether there's an IC path that would recognize their value instead.

It's also useful during hiring: if a candidate's last promotion pushed them past their skill set, that's worth probing — not to disqualify, but to understand what role they would actually thrive in.
